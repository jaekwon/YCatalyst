Hi all,

I want to share what I've been thinking in as few words as possible and get
out of the way.

   1. The site shouldn't be vote-based like HN, Reddit, Digg, etc. Rather,
   it should follow MetaFilter and Lambda The Ultimate in being a sort of
   community-run group blog. There might be an element of voting/flagging,
   particularly around comments, but posts should be moderated or picked by
   editors.
   2. A good set of initial moderators and users is key. I'm sure we all
   know smart folks to invite.
   3. Keep invites closed at first. Start by inviting only by referral.
   Gradually open it up a bit more.
   4. Don't hesitate to charge. It keeps the spammers out. Anywhere between
   $1 - $10 for an account is totally reasonable. Pinboard.in does this,
   MetaFilter does it, Something Awful does it. The precedent is set.
   5. Filter out known-vapid domains. Simply don't allow users to submit
   links to TechCrunch, Mashable, Reddit, etc. It might even be worth scraping
   the top stories on Techmeme a couple times an hour to populate a submission
   blacklist. Without this restriction, the site will devolve into general tech
   news.
   6. Thinking about how the moderation tools are going to work is more
   important than how the site will look to users.
   7. Don't get too clever with the algorithms and social/personalization
   features. This is more about cultivating the right community than inventing
   a magical personalized tech wire service.

As for the business/organizational aspect of the project:

   - It would be great to see it run as a real non-profit. Registering as a
   non-profit takes some time and money, though, so I don't expect this to
   happen early on.
   - Advertising is okay to cover costs, as long as the ads are tasteful and
   clearly separated from the content.
   - Any revenue beyond what's needed to cover costs should be donated to
   tech-related charities. Everyone can feel good about that.
   - Don't run this as an open source project. You'll end up with Advogato
   or something like that.

Technology:

   - Pick Rails or Django. There's nothing fancy or demanding about a
   content-management system. Just pick a web framework that lots of people
   know how to use and make it happen.
   - Host in the cloud.

Curious to know what you all are thinking.

Hello everyone,

I am very intrigued by the idea of limiting the number of reads
everyone gets. The first questions that spring to mind are:
  * will you be allowed to comment on articles you did not read?
  * How can we apply the same kind of incentives to comments?
  * What if (and this is common for me) I read something elsewhere,
and the come to the site for discussion?

This is related to a bigger question I have. What do you want the pace
of the site to be? The turnover on HN is relatively rapid, though
slower than reddit, whereas metafilter has the same articles on its
front page for a few days. Do we want the philosophy of the site to be
"post anything you find interesting and see what everyone likes" or
more metafiltery where individuals post very rarely and only when they
(and the moderators) think something is exceptional? On HN you can
always find something new to read, do we want that to be possible
here? Would it be bad if a day went by and nothing new showed up?

If we want the site to be slowly paced, I think that will answer some
of our technical questions right away: be mostly like metafilter. They
have had a large and strong community going for a long time. I think I
am mostly leaning toward this option. (Though I think Petar does have
a good point about how we will find good moderators with enough time
on their hands to moderate)

If we want the users to be more of a filter, then I think we would be
well served considering some more algorithmic solutions to these
problems. PageRank (or its ilk) interest me for this, too. Votes for
users (by proxy with votes for their comments? or perhaps better, you
can "follow" people who interest you?) could give their votes more
power?

Or maybe it isn't as black and white as I think.

Regardless, I'm personally interested in how we can make the
monolithic community more accessible to individuals. On HN, if I have
an interesting exchange with someone in the comments, I have no easy
way of keeping track of them going forward. If I later see comments by
the same person, I will usually have forgotten that their particular
username was the one I was conversing with a week before. What would
happen if I could have more context with people's comments? A little
"Hey you've upvoted this person's comments a few times" highlighting
might go a long way toward making the community more real, less
faceless.

Cheers,

MacRae

Just to clarify (another way of looking at this idea):

Everyone is essentially a human curator in their act of
writing and "re-tweeting". You get to choose which curators
you like.

Since curators interested in the same topic will follow each other
and re-tweet articles of common interest, they will naturally
form sub-communities where articles reach everyone (despite
the FIXED number of curators that each person follows).

This also puts a natural and human limit on how many articles
go by your eyes.

The FIXED part is key, so that you don't get the twitter effect
where people follow 1000 other people. (They don't actually
read their tweets, rather do it in hopes of reciprocated following
and unsolicited self-promotion).

I am jazzed about this idea.

I like the idea of following people, but I don't think it should be what
decides what I see.  I just want to be notified when they comment and that
kind of stuff. Personally I have pretty little interest in limiting my news
sources numerically, it's a naive filter.

MacRae said above:

"Regardless, I'm personally interested in how we can make the
monolithic community more accessible to individuals. On HN, if I have
an interesting exchange with someone in the comments, I have no easy
way of keeping track of them going forward. If I later see comments by
the same person, I will usually have forgotten that their particular
username was the one I was conversing with a week before. What would
happen if I could have more context with people's comments? A little
"Hey you've upvoted this person's comments a few times" highlighting
might go a long way toward making the community more real, less
faceless."

That's what I want: HN with more social community features (in terms of
product).  HN, focused just on tech, with a friendlier interface and more
thought given to social features.

I'm a strong believer in launching quickly and iterating, so a basic version
of my idea would be starting with HN as a base:

- Add real user profiles with avatars and such.  Profile should include a
section about which technologies people use
- Some kind of reputation system (preferably based on real life
accomplishments somehow.  One quick idea is the profile has a concept of
"brags" - you would add a "brag" [ie: i created google.com] and people could
up and down vote it.  like a very rough resume or something)
- Add following/friends list - I just want a social stream page of all my
friends comments.
- Add content specific subsections
- Add 1+ light-moderators/discussion leaders for each subsection.  This
helps anti spam and chicken and egg problem.

I think those small features would be a good basic start towards fixing the
problem because:

- One of the main issues is content relevance, subsections helps that, as
does the central theming of the site
- Subsections also allows for better community building.  It allows more
specific groups of people to coexist, and it allows these groups to be
smaller.  At the same time having a general aggregated home page gives us
the best of both worlds
- HN's community is killer for information, but it can be a little like
talking to the comic book guy at work who knows everything but is a prick.
I think we just need to be slightly less robotic and cold, which goes back
to avatars and real user profiles.

I do think doing much more will have diminishing returns until massive scale
(at which point bring on the algos).  But right now and for the foreseeable
future community is the only thing that matters.  If we implement this and
can get a fraction of HN's technical side on there I think that would be a
really great start.

Also please introduce yourselves on the other thread so we can really start
to get an idea of who can contribute what.

Your sentiment, that the "idea" will feel different to the user is valid.
This is because, like on twitter, people would feel that they don't have
the same view as everyone else. And this may be a psychological block
or irritation.

Here's another twist (again, I am not insisting here, just having fun with
the thought process):

Imagine everyone starts off with the same view: All articles are seen
in your stream. But every time you vote on an article, it has the implication
that, just for you, ever so slightly the articles of the author you up-voted will
tend to come up higher in the list.

So everyone still sees everything, but the ordering will be slightly different.

This incentives people to not vote for articles today, whose likes they may
not want to see in the future.

This is not an irreversible process, since you can always shift your ranking
in any direction by voting more often on articles of the type that you care about
in the long term.

Not to optimize early, but scaling to any real number would be insanely
difficult.

I want to reintroduce the idea that math is great for working with larger
groups, but that in the early days community will drive it.  The best algo
in the world wont fix a crap community, but a great community will beat a
crap algo (even the "new" page of HN is really pretty good, and it's 100%
unsorted, could be spam, but because of the community who is submitting even
it is pretty high quality).

We can talk all day about algorithms, or we can go and get some data and
then analyze it.

So let's focus on the product, and getting enough content that it actually
needs to be sorted before we start solving the problem of how to sort it
(though I understand that is the more attractive problem, especially for a
csailer ;-D).  Either way, by launching early with a simple idea, we'll be
getting really helpful data for answering this question.
